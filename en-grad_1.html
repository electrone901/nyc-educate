Grad School

Introduction: Minorities and Graduate Education

Nothing (not even immigration status) should stop a college graduate from pursuing a post-graduate degree if that is the best path toward the future career they want. Unfortunately, there are many studies that analyze unequal access to higher education and the under-representation of minority students in many degree programs and their correspondent careers (See Here).

This lack of access is obviously related to structural issues, such as the expenses involved in pursuing a graduate degree and the unequal quality of education in the US, but it also relates to other phenomena, for example, the subtle ways in which minorities are often channeled into lower-earning, less prestigious careers (see here for example) .

There are further, more subtle and less discussed factors that contribute to this disparity, including the stress involved in constantly striving to ‘overcome one’s circumstances,’ the emotional weight of constantly being considered an ideal ‘representative’ of a ‘community’ (and the related fear of ‘failing our community’), and the different threshold that minority and low income students sometimes have for the risk taking involved in pursuits of a high education (taking out loans, deferring income, spending years seemingly doing ‘nothing’ but study—a stress even more acutely felt when someone is the first in their family or social group to undertake such an endeavor). The financial, social, and emotional barriers to post-graduate careers are multifold. Encouragingly, many thousands of minority students overcome these barriers each year.
