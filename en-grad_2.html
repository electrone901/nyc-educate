Deciding whether a Post-graduate Degree Is Right for You

Perhaps the first question a potential post-grad applicant should consider is: is a post-graduate degree the best or only way to pursue my dreams?

It is tempting to think about this as a purely financial decision. Forbes, not surprisingly, offers an article on calculating the ROI (return on investment) for a graduate degree, and they mention important considerations such as deferred earnings, career prospects after graduation, and retirement possibilities: See Here

However, be careful not to think of ROI as merely your future earnings minus expenses. Consider whether the degree helps you pursue something you are passionate about: a topic, cause, a practice such as teaching or researching, or just a dream you’ve always had.

With a consideration of both your dreams and the financial implications of a post-graduate degree in mind, it is crucial to do research about the career or degree in question. General questions include: What kind of degree do I need to pursue my dreams (what area, what level of study—masters, doctoral, etc.)? What is the job market like for this career / for current degree holders, and how is that market projected to change? How much do degree holders earn? How competitive is the job market? Where are new jobs located? What are your career prospects without this degree?

More specifically you should research individual programs and schools: Where are degree programs offered and how do they differ from each other (there can be significant difference between programs)? How long does it take, on average, to earn a degree, and what steps are involved? How are current students funding their studies? What is the graduation rate? Who are the faculty (especially those involved in your specific area of interest) and what are they like?

You can find much of this information at public or college libraries, career centers, and on the Internet. However, a key part of your research should be actually taking to people. It’s particularly important to interview current and past students in a program you are considering—they are among the most important resources available to help you make a good initial decision and to successfully complete the program should you enroll (their advice and tips will be invaluable).

You should also interview key faculty members and/or other professionals who work in your prospective field. Faculty members need to value you enough to want to work with you (their individual assessment of your application and their desire to work with you are among the most important factors in being accepted into a program and offered funding), but you also need to consider whether you want to work with them and put your career in their hands. Consider what their strengths and weaknesses are: Research? Publishing? Teaching? Mentoring? Toxic faculty and especially committee members can hinder even the brightest student’s career. If the school/department or profession doesn’t value the fact that you need to do as much due diligence as they do, you might want to consider how much you can actually achieve in that environment.

Students are often encouraged to take at least a year off between undergraduate and graduate studies. This time can be extremely useful for exploring new options, trying different career paths, or just taking a break from a topic, even if it’s one you love, to experiment with something different. This time off can also give you time to do research. Just remember to stay in touch with the faculty members, employers, or other people you met during college to get their letters of recommendation if you do apply to grad school at a later date (an occasional card, letter, or email often suffices).

Armed with research and after careful consideration, you should be able to honestly answer questions about what grad school means to you:
  • Is the time (and deferred earnings) worth it for me?
  • Is the extra money (or the hassle involved in securing funding) worth it for me?
  • Am I passionate about this career?
  • Is there some other career, topic, or experience I’ve always wanted to try?
  • Am I considering grad school because I can’t think of anything else to do, or because my parents or teachers expect me to do it?
