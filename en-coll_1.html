College

You’re in College. Now What?

There are lots of challenges a student faces on the way to a college degree, but often these challenges only intensify for students trying again after having dropped out. Once recent study has found that while 54.1 percent of first-time students completed their degree nationally, only 33.7 percent of non-first-time students achieved the same goal (See Here). If a student wants to re-enroll after having dropping out, they might find themselves having to re-take classes, or losing credits they accumulated in one institution of they can be transferred to another. It’s often to a student’s advantage—once they start a college program—to stay in it until they’ve graduated.

It is important to understand all your rights as a student as well as the laws that govern New York’s public educational system. (These rights and responsibilities are described in greater detail in the “Additional Ways to Graduate Directory” available in English here and in Spanish here).

Of course, not everyone chooses the right college the first time around. Sometimes a student’s financial situation, personal life or interests change, and in theses cases switching to a new college or program can be a smart decision. However, all new college students should approach their first year as aware as possible of the challenges they will encounter so they can prepare themselves and find the support and resources necessary to make it to graduation.

There are many diverse, often interrelated factors that lead to poor college outcomes (low grades, graduating late, or not graduating at all). This list shouldn’t deter you—it is meant to help you to anticipate some of these very common roadblocks and plan accordingly:
  • Money: Many students are unaware of the many expenses incurred by a college student. Books (especially course textbooks) and course materials can be extremely costly, and a student might incur new travel expenses or higher food costs. Students might feel pressure to spend money on clothes or entertainment to keep with other kids at school, or they might just find themselves spending more money as a result of having less parental oversight. Jobs offered on campus or through college work-study programs might not be enough to cover these expenses.
  • Work: related to the above point, many students work long hours to support themselves or their families. They might find themselves unable to finish their assignments on time or participate in group projects. They might find themselves exhausted from the combined demands of school and work.
  • Lack of family support: many students come from families who don’t support their pursuit of a college degree. Families might see college as an unnecessary expense or even as a waste of time, especially if a student could be earning what seems like “good money” entering the labor market now. Families that support the idea of going to college might not be able to offer financial, logistical, or emotional support, or they might not know how to reassure students when they are facing challenges.
  • Loneliness: Many first-time college students experience feeling “like a fish out of water,” overwhelmed by cultural differences or by other students who seem to “know what they are doing.” Students often miss they family, friends and teachers they left behind in their former schools/lives (this is even especially acute in the case of students who move away from home).
  • Academics: Students might themselves underprepared academically or just unsure of their teachers’ expectations. A few bad grades might lead them to believe they weren’t “cut out” for college.
  • New responsibilities: Sometimes, going to college also coincides with having new responsibilities--taking on new chores or tasks that other adults (a parent, grandparent, teacher) previously performed for you. You might have to cook, clean, or do laundry for yourself. You have to organize your own schedule, choose your own classes, and manage your own course load. Students are expected to be self-starters and punctual. They need to manage distractions without anyone necessarily looking over their shoulder. Also, they need to problem solve in new ways and find the answers questions such as: how does my GPA affect my financial aid, will tuition rates change next year, what are the requirements for graduation, and how will my choices now affect my career prospects after graduation?
