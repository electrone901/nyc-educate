Key advice: When choosing a kindergarten program, don’t forget to think ahead. Consider both the quality of the kindergarten program itself and whether it helps channel your child into a quality elementary school program that will meet your child’s needs as he or she grows.

For students 17-21 who are eligible but not currently enrolled in high school (who un-enrolled or who are new to NYC) or who want to leave high school:

Finishing high school and continuing your education is more important than ever before. According to the Pew Research Center, today’s young adults with only a high school diploma or less “are more likely to be unemployed and [spend] more time searching for a job” (Pew Research Center, February, 2014, “The Rising Cost of Not Going to College”). Deciding to obtain a high school diploma is also an important way to exercise your rights as a resident of New York (rights which are yours regardless of your immigration status). Compulsory school attendance in the US developed over time and for many reasons, but is today linked to the belief that education prepares people as citizens, develops their values, and increases prosperity on both the individual and community level. The law also reflects the contemporary belief that education is a child’s fundamental right.

It is important to understand all your rights as a student as well as the laws that govern New York’s public educational system. (These rights and responsibilities are described in greater detail in the “Additional Ways to Graduate Directory” available in English here and in Spanish here).

For example: If you are under 21 and a NYC resident you have:

  • the right to a free public school education or a high school diploma
  • the right to a bilingual education or English as a second language program as provided by law if you are determined to an English Language Learner
  • students with disabilities who have been determined to be in need of special education are entitled to a free appropriate public education from age 3 until age 21, as provided by law
  • the right to enroll in high school at any time, until the end of the school year in which you turn 21
  • the right not to be involuntarily transferred to a non-diploma granting or part-time program
  • the right not to be barred from attending classes solely due to age (unless you are over 21 years old), lack of credits, poor grades, truancy, irregular attendance, or failure to pass Regents or RCT examinations
  • the right to request, at any time, an evaluation to determine whether a student needs special education services

    • Legal responsibilities:
  • If you are 17 years of age or younger and will turn 17 on or after July 1, you are legally required to remain in school full-time until the end of that school year, unless you have completed a high school or equivalency diploma.

    • Students are eligible to attend high school until the age of 21 (you can go to school until the end of that school year in which you turn 21). However, these students have additional options available to them for obtaining a high school diploma. These options can be explained at a Referral Center for High School Alternatives. Located in each borough, they are open Monday through Friday from 8:30 a.m. to 2:30 p.m. on days that schools are open.

    At Referral Centers, counselors can help you:

  • Find out how to obtain an high school diploma, or enroll in a high school equivalency program
  • Understand your high school transcript
  • Get referrals for childcare, social work, drug treatment and housing;
  • Learn about other programs
  • Take a placement test if necessary

    • The website for NYC DOE Referral Centers is located at here. The page can be translated into Spanish or many other languages by clicking on the language option in the upper right hand corner of the webpage.

    Generally speaking, the options they will provide to you are:

  • Stay at or returning to your previous high school –Depending on your age and school history (credit accumulation and Regents examinations) – staying in or returning to your home school may be the best option for you.
  • Enrolling in a new high school: for more on enrolling as a new student, see the section “How to enroll in public school (for students new to NYC, grades 9-12) or find a high school alternative (for students 15-21)”
  • Enroll in another school or program that can help you graduate (see the section the follows): your age and personal considerations (such as whether you can take classes during the day or evenings only) will be among the factors that help determine your options for these programs. Options include:
  • Transfer schools: these are designed to be are small, full-time high schools designed to re-engage students who have dropped out or who have fallen behind in credits. You must be 15-21 years old and have completed at least one year of high school. You apply directly to transfer schools through their admissions process. Since admissions criteria vary, it is best to contact the school directly. For general information and links to brochures here.
  • Young Adult Borough Centers: these are evening academic programs designed for high school students who are behind in credit or considering dropping out for other reasons (such as having adult responsibilities in the daytime). You must be 17.5-21 years old (having completed the school year in which they turn 17, noting that the school year begins July 1st), have a minimum of 17 course credits, be in the fifth year (or more) of high school, be enrolled or become enrolled in a NYC high school. For general information and links to brochures in multiple languages here.
  • Pathways to Graduation: a free, full-time program run by the DOE that helps students earn their high school equivalency diploma. Students are expected to complete a certain number of classroom hours in the program before they can take the Readiness Exam. The program also offers college and career coaching, and students are required to complete a portfolio of elements such as a resume, cover letter, personal statement and an individual learning plan. Downloadable brochures in various languages: here.
  • Learning to Work: this is not an equivalency program but a support program available to some Transfer Schools and at all Young Adult Borough Centers. The goal is to help students overcome the obstacles that stand in the way of obtaining a high school diploma and to develop a path toward post-secondary educations and career option. This program gives students academic support services, college and career preparation, paid internships and other skills preparation. See here.
  • The School of Cooperative Technical Education: a free half-day vocational program that offers students hands on training in a variety of trade areas such as building trades, health careers, information technology and service industries. The program is for students ages 16 to 21 years of age and attend either certain 130 traditional high schools (in 11th to 12th grades), Pathways to Graduation programs (described below) or as post-graduates who have already obtained a diploma. Students with IEPs can attend. For more information and for details on how to apply see: here.
  • LYFE: a DOE program that provides free, high-quality childcare to children of parents enrolled in a NYC DOE school or program. Available at over 30 site in NYC, LYFE also offers counseling, academic guidance and advocacy services. More information is available in a variety of languages here.

    • For students 21 and over (ineligible for enrollment in a public high school) who want to earn a high school diploma

    Students 21 and over can still access free city educational programs to obtain their high school equivalency diplomas—regardless of their immigration status. This section will focus on Adult and Continuing Education programs run by the NYC Department of Education. However, many libraries, community centers, faith-based organizations, community colleges, and other local service providers throughout NYC offer free classes that prepare students for the high school equivalency test, also called the TASC (Test Assessing Secondary Completion). Some of these other test prep service providers will be listed in the additional resources section at the end of this document.

    You might hear the High School Equivalency test referred to as the “GED.” “GED” is the brand name of the high school equivalency test that was administered prior to 2014. It has been replaced by the TASC exam in New York State. (The change does not affect students who previously took and passed the GED to earn their high school equivalency diploma.

    Free NYC DOE sponsored classes that prepare you for the TASC exam are offered by the Office of Adult and Continuing Education (OACE) during both day and evening, Monday through Saturday at many locations throughout NYC (public schools, libraries, community centers and places of worship). However, students first have to go to an orientation and take an assessment test at one of several Adult Learning Centers (registration locations) located in Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens and the Bronx (classes themselves are taught at a wider variety of locations). Registration locations and numbers are listed here . These locations also offer other services such as case management, student orientation, and goal setting workshops. Locations should be contacted ahead of time to find out when an orientation is being held. If you have trouble reaching your desired Adult Learning Center or encounter other problems, you can call the OACE central office at 718-638-2635 (prompts are in English) or 311.

    Know your rights! Students registering in a NYC DOE classes English as a Second Language, Adult Basic Education, or High School Equivalency programs do not need to provide their social security number. Students who register in Career and Technical Education classes (below) that lead to certification may be asked to provide a social security number or other documents to verify their identity for job placement.

    Test of Basic Adult Education (TBAE) is required of all first time applicants. It is a math and reading test to assess academic readiness and appropriate course placement. If an applicants results fall below the requirements, they will be offered literacy classes to help them reach the level of readiness required for their original course of choice.

    “Distance learning,” a program for students who could take classes in person, has been discontinued. However, adults in this situation might still try calling the OACE central office at 718-638-2635 (prompts are in English) or 311 to ask for assistance or explore other options.

    More information on the NYC DOE High School Equivalency programs is available here .

    Students 21 and over interested in other adult education classes

    The NYC DOE Office of Adult and Continuing Education offers free adult literacy programs, English as a Second Language, and Career and Technical Education classes for free. English as a Second Language and Adult Basic Education students do not need to provide their social security number. Students who register in Career and Technical Education classes that lead to certification may be asked to provide a social security number or other documents to verify their identity for job placement.

    The classes offered by the NYC DOE are not your only option for continuing your education. Other organizations offer similar classes for free or for a fee (some of these resources are described below in the “ additional resources ” section). Do not assume that a fee-based class is any better than a free class. There have been cases of for-profit schools that have defrauded their students, charging fees for very poor or non-existent services. Other for-profit schools offer high quality instruction with additional materials, supports and a wide variety of class times. You need to be a careful consumer of these services, whether you pay a fee or not.

    When possible, before registering and committing your time (and/or money) to any program, ask to sit in on a class to see what it is like. Consider whether the class requires class participation, group work, extra reading, or homework. Before you enroll you should ask what is expected outside class hours (such as projects or research). Teachers should be certified, experienced, approachable, respectful, and understanding of their students needs. Having this knowledge before you begin can help you successfully complete the program. If you do not like the class, the teacher, center, or any other feature of the program, try another location or a different school/program altogether. You have many options available to you, and not all programs are the same.

    To attend a class given by the Office of Adult and Continuing Education, students first have to go to an orientation and take an assessment test at one of several Adult Learning Centers (registration locations) located in Brooklyn, Manhattan, Queens and the Bronx (classes themselves are taught at a wider variety of locations). Registration locations and numbers are listed at: here. These locations also offer other services such as case management, student orientation, and goal setting workshops. Locations should be contacted ahead of time to find out when an orientation is being held. If you have trouble reaching your desired Adult Learning Center or encounter other problems, you can call the OACE central office at 718-638-2635 (prompts are in English) or 311.

    Some OACE programs (in addition the to high school equivalency preparation classes described above) include:

    Adult Basic Education (ABE) Instruction - skills related to adult literacy needs, educational goals, and personal interests. Adults enrolled in ABE classes can advance to high school equivalency preparation and vocational training.

    English for Speakers of Other Languages (ESOL) Instruction - basic language skills, as well as the academic and civic skills they need to successfully complete higher education or job training programs.

    Career and Technical Training – courses to acquire technical, trade, construction, or entrepreneurial skills (a high school diploma may be required for some of these classes). Classes cover many topics in many general areas such as:

  • Health Trades – Training to become a Licensed Practical Nursing, Certified Nurse Assistant, Phlebotomist, and in Medial Billing and Coding,
  • Information Technology – classes teaching software programs, networking, security, web design
  • Construction
  • General Trades – courses in carpentry, air conditioning and refrigeration, boiler and building management, and culinary arts/food preparation, electrical, plumbing
  • Early Childhood Education
